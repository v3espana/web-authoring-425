A Quick Look Into One of Our Climbing Days

Pre-Climbing The Climbing Session Highlights

The Pre-Game and Warm-Up

Wednesdays are our go-to climbing days, and not just by coincidence. Gravity Vault Princeton offers a student discount every Wednesday, making it the perfect excuse to step away from classes, assignments, and all our other weekly responsibilities. What started as a convenient deal quickly became a weekly tradition. A typical climbing session starts in our apartment, where we do our weekly pre-workout: grabbing climbing bags, filling water bottles, and making sure we’re fed and energized (I usually scarf down a Rice Krispies treat and a Chomps meat stick). According to Skratch Labs, it is important to time your meals and hydration before your climbing sessions, and that even light fueling during a session can make a huge difference in endurance and performance! Though I’m not tracking macros, I’ve learned that showing up prepared is the best way to have a successful climbing experience.

Vincent Hopping On His First Climb of the Day

The drive itself is a big part of our day. We pile into a car and take Route 1 down towards Princeton, which is usually about 40 minutes, give or take. We’re always talking about which projects were sending, joking about past climbing days, and figuring out who else is on their way. Once we get to the gym, the routine begins. We sign in, grab our day passes, and stash our belongings in the cubbies. Then comes what we’ve learned as one of the most important parts of climbing: the warm-up. It’s easy to overlook stretching when you’re eager to hop on the wall, but over time (and through some injuries), we’ve realized how essential it is. In this article, Lattice Training explains the importance of flexibility in climbing and how the different kinds of stretching interact with each other, both on and off the wall. The emphasis is that proper stretching and warming-up, whether that is hopping on the hang board or flashing some V0’s, help prevent injury and improve overall performance, making it a non-negotiable step in our climbing routine. After that, it’s game time. We all head to the climbs that we were eyeing as soon as we walked in, usually pointing out routes fun or exciting new route sets. Everyone kind of splits off at first, but we always manage to circle back to watch and help each other out.

The Climbing Session

Vincent on the Top-Rope Wall

This is when the real process of climbing begins. Some climbs go down in one try (what we call a flash), while others keep us stuck for most of the session. We miss our holds, slip off footholds, and get stuck halfway up the wall. But that’s part of what makes climbing so satisfying: the constant cycle of trying, failing adjusting, and trying again. Every failed attempt turns into a new quick discussion on the ground about what went wrong and what to try next. Sometimes, it’s a small adjustment, like changing foot placement or grabbing the hold differently, and other times it’s completely rethinking the route. One of the most important skills we’ve started developing is route reading. Before even touching the wall, we take a moment to study the climb from the ground up and try to piece together a path to the top. At first, the wall looks like a language we don’t understand, like a random scatter of colorful holds. Over time, you start noticing patterns: which holds are for your hands versus feet, or how your body might need to shift between positions. Like always, route reading doesn’t always go as planned, as saying you can do something is a lot easier than being able to do it. Those slight hiccups mid-climb are always important lessons, though, as they teach us to slow down, reassess, and adapt instead of just muscling through climbs.

Vincent and Annie Playing Ping Pong

As the session goes on, the small wins start to stand out more. Someone finally finishes a route they’ve been working on, or someone else gets just a little farther than they did before. Even those small improvements feel like big accomplishments. They’re also what keep us motivated to come back the next week and try again. Eventually, the fatigue sets in. Our grip weakens, our forearms start to burn, and we know that it’s nearing the end of our session. But even then, we’re not quite done yet. Before leaving, we always have to play at least a couple of games of table tennis before we leave. It’s become a bit of an unspoken rule (especially for Annie), and for us to end the day on something a little more lighthearted before heading to our post-climbing dinner. While they do that, I might rent a harness and hit a top-rope, since there's truly nothing like the feeling of making it all the way to the top of the ceiling (if my arms can last that long)! On our way out, we’re tired in the best way possible, already talking about the climbs we’re determined to send next time. At its core, this routine is exactly what The Crux Corner is all about. It’s not just about document climbs or reviewing gyms, it’s about showing the full experience: the preparation, the wins, the failures, and most importantly, the people that you share it with. Our Wednesday sessions are a snapshot of that larger idea, and that’s what inspired us to start building this platform in the first place: to provide a space for people who see climbing the way we do and to make those stepping into it for the first time feel a little more confident.
